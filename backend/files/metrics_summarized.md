1. Injection outcomes
We feed the AI wrong answers at four specific moments. Did the candidate catch them, go back to check, ignore them, or put the wrong answer in their memo? This is the whole product thesis in four data points. If you can only measure one thing, it's this.
2. Exhibit reopen after AI response
After the AI tells them something, did they go back to the case to check it? This is the purest verification signal we have. It's behavioral, not subjective — either they opened the exhibit or they didn't. You can't fake it without knowing we're watching for it.
3. Paste events into memo
Did they copy the AI's words directly into their recommendation. If yes, the memo isn't theirs. Simple, clean, impossible to misinterpret.
4. Prompt quality
We read every message they sent to the AI and score it. Did they ask "what should I think about this deal" or did they ask "the growth has decelerated from 58% to 28% — does the 22.7x multiple hold up given that trajectory?" One of those shows thinking. The other outsources it. This is the best proxy we have for how someone actually reasons.
5. Memo propagation
Did the wrong answers we injected survive into their final recommendation. This is the business consequence signal. A candidate who writes "NRR is top quartile" in their memo just made a mistake that would cost a real firm real money. That's what we're measuring.
6. Memo quality
Did they write a real recommendation with specific numbers, actual comparables, and a clear position — or generic language that could apply to any company. This separates genuine synthesis from polished AI output.
7. Time before first AI message
Did they read the case before asking for help. Candidates who go straight to the AI without reading the material aren't using AI to amplify their thinking — they're using it to replace it entirely.
8. Cross-signal pattern label
After everything above is computed, Claude assigns them a name. Ghost-Writer, Sophisticated Verifier, Competent Cargo-Cult. This is just a synthesis of all the other signals into something a hiring manager can immediately understand and remember. It's not a new measurement — it's a translation.
